# Training Framework

The conceptual model behind `ai-driver-loops`. This document explains *why* the toolkit is shaped the way it is.

For installation and usage, see the [README](../README.md).

---

## Problem

If you're an AI-native developer (using Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot, etc. daily), you've probably felt:

- Objectively producing more, subjectively standing still.
- AI gives you working solutions, but you can't articulate what *you* learned.
- You want to "master AI" but can't isolate what that skill actually is.
- Anxiety about lack of progress.

The root cause: **AI-mediated development eats the "I'm getting stronger" feedback signal.** Your skill at orchestrating AI grows, but the muscle-memory loop ("I solved this myself") never fires.

## North Star

> When AI drifts off-track, you **detect, locate, and correct** it — fast.
> The autopilot is its job. The steering wheel is yours.

This is the skill to train. Not "code without AI" — that's anti-tool. The goal is to *steer* AI better.

## The skill, decomposed

| Layer | Content |
|---|---|
| 1. Domain priors | What "right" looks like in your head — the baseline for detecting drift |
| 2. AI failure-mode intuition | Knowing where AI typically fails and how |
| 3. Critical reading speed | Spotting the issue in 30s, not line-by-line review |
| 4. Surgical correction | Pulling AI back in one sentence, not rewriting |

Most people stall at layers 1–2. Layers 3–4 are practice; they unlock once priors are deep enough.

## Four training loops

### Loop A — AI deviation log (daily)

Every time you catch AI making a mistake, log a 30-second entry: scenario / what's wrong / how you caught it / category.

After ~30 entries you have a personal AI failure-mode taxonomy. More importantly, you can *see* "last week I missed this kind, this week I caught it" — that's the missing feedback signal.

→ Tool: `ai-deviation-log` skill (dual-mode: quick-capture during flow, batch-process after).

### Loop B — Predict before reveal (daily, mental habit)

Before AI produces an answer, predict in one sentence: "It will do X, will fail at Y." Then compare. Hit rate going up = your model is aligning with reality.

→ **Not tooled deliberately.** A hook fires too late by timing (you've already submitted the prompt). Tooling B turns it into hindsight, which defeats the practice. This stays as a mental habit.

### Loop C — Forced critique (daily, with soft reminder)

Take an AI output that "looks fine" and force yourself to find 3 problems with it — even if you have to stretch. Trains active scrutiny. Most failures happen because the answer looked OK.

→ Tool: `c-loop-reminder.ps1` hook. Soft, sampled (1/4) reminder on Claude Code's `Stop` event. Not a forcing function — a nudge that survives habituation by being rare.

### Loop D — Deep domain sprint (every two weeks)

Pick one narrow domain (e.g., "HNSW index for vector retrieval"). Run four sequential stages, no skipping:

1. **D-1 Socratic learning** — AI quizzes you concept by concept, doesn't lecture.
2. **D-2 Cross-check against non-AI sources** — official docs, papers, source code. Spot AI's omissions and oversimplifications.
3. **D-3 Mandatory artifact** — toy implementation / teardown / Q&A clinic. Must be written by *you*, AI can only critique.
4. **D-4 Reverse grill** — AI plays interviewer, 10 progressively harder questions, scores you.

→ Tool: `deep-domain-sprint` skill (state machine, blocks skipping).

## Progress KPIs (weekly, 30 seconds)

| Metric | Direction |
|---|---|
| AI errors caught this week | Up |
| When caught (before / during / after output) | Earlier |
| First-prompt usable rate | Up |
| New concepts I understand "AI-free" | ≥ 2 per week |

These together = objective evidence you're holding the steering wheel. More reliable than "I feel like I'm growing."

## Design choices (why these shapes)

| Loop | Tooling | Why |
|---|---|---|
| A | Dual-mode skill | Debug flow is sacred — capture must be ≤3 seconds. Quality fields ("how I caught it") get filled in batch later, not generated by AI |
| B | None | Hook timing is structurally wrong. Forced tooling = hindsight = defeats purpose |
| C | Hook with 1/4 sampling | Soft nudge survives habituation. Active skill invocation would be too heavy |
| D | State-machine skill | Fixed ritual that benefits from enforcement against skipping |

## Core principle

> AI stays in the loop. Your brain stays in the driver's seat.

- Don't bypass AI (that's anti-tool)
- Don't be fed by AI (that's premium-grade doomscrolling)
- "Use AI deeper / use AI correctly" is the path
